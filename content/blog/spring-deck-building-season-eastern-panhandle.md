If a new deck has been living on your to-do list for a couple of years, here's the honest truth about timing: the best time to build was last season, and the second-best time is the moment before everyone else in Martinsburg and Charles Town has the same idea. Deck demand runs from the first warm weekend straight through fall, and reputable crews book up fast.

Here's how to think about timing, materials, permits, and — most importantly — how to hire someone who won't leave you with a wobbly cautionary tale.

## Timing: Earlier Is Always Better Than "When I Get Around To It"

A few things actually depend on the calendar:

- **The ground has to cooperate.** Deck footings need to be set below the frost line (typically 30 inches in West Virginia). Once the soil has thawed and stabilized — which it has by late spring and stays that way through fall — conditions are ideal for pouring footings that'll last decades.
- **You want to *use* the thing.** A typical deck runs two to four weeks from permit to final walkthrough, depending on complexity. Start the conversation early in the season and you're grilling on it by summer. Call in mid-summer and you're joining a backlog — most good crews in the Martinsburg and Charles Town area are booked solid by then, and "your deck's ready... for next spring" is a sad sentence.

Bottom line: whenever you're reading this, the move is to get on a reputable builder's schedule sooner rather than later.

## What Decking Actually Costs

Lumber and composite pricing tends to climb as demand peaks, so locking in early helps the wallet too. Here's the honest comparison:

- **Pressure-Treated Pine:** the budget pick, roughly $15–$25 per square foot installed. Looks sharp freshly stained — but "freshly stained" is a recurring subscription in our humid climate, every one to two years.
- **Composite (Trex, TimberTech):** higher upfront at roughly $30–$55 per square foot installed, and then basically nothing for 25+ years. No staining, no splintering, no rot, no annual weekend lost to a sander.
- **Hardwood (Ipe, Cumaru):** premium looks and serious durability at the top price point. For the homeowner who wants a showpiece and isn't flinching at the invoice.

For most Eastern Panhandle families, composite has become the default. The upfront premium pays for itself within a few years once you tally up all the staining you didn't do and all the Saturdays you got back.

## Permits and Planning — Don't Wing This Part

Both Jefferson County and Berkeley County require building permits for new deck construction. This is not the corner to cut. An unpermitted deck turns into a problem exactly when you least want one — at closing, when you're trying to sell — and your homeowner's insurance may not cover an injury on a structure that technically isn't supposed to exist.

A licensed contractor handles the permit for you, including plans that meet current International Residential Code (IRC) requirements.

**Key code points worth knowing:**

- Railing is required on any deck surface 30 inches or more above grade.
- Balusters can't be spaced more than 4 inches apart — the famous "soda can test." If a can fits through, so does a toddler, and the spacing's too wide.
- The ledger board connection to your house needs proper lag screws or through-bolts *with flashing*. This is the connection that holds the deck to the house, so we'd prefer it stay enthusiastic about its job.

## What to Ask Before You Hire

A deck is a real investment, and not every "deck guy" is the same. Before you sign anything, your contractor should answer a confident *yes* to all of these:

- Are you licensed and insured in West Virginia?
- Will you pull the building permit and schedule the inspections?
- Can you show references from deck projects finished in the last 12 months?
- Do you offer a workmanship warranty on top of the manufacturer's material warranty?

If someone gets squirmy on any of these, that's not your builder. Keep looking.
