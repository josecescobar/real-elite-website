Summer in the Eastern Panhandle is afternoon thunderstorms rolling off the Allegheny ridges, the occasional severe wind event down the valleys, and every few years a hail storm that works over a whole neighborhood at once like it's running a route. When one of those events finds your roof, the hours and days afterward matter — both for protecting your home and for getting a fair insurance claim.

Here's the straight playbook: what to do first, how to tell whether you actually have damage, how roof insurance claims really work in West Virginia, Maryland, and Virginia, and how to spot the storm-chasing crews that show up the next morning with smiles too rehearsed to trust.

## The first 48 hours: what to do (and what not to)

**Stay off the roof.** A wet or storm-damaged roof is one of the most dangerous places on your property. Loose shingles, hidden soft spots, and slick surfaces send people to the ER every storm season. Document from the ground and let a professional get up there.

**Document everything from the ground.** Take wide photos of each side of the house and zoom in on anything obvious — shingles in the yard, a dented gutter, a branch on the roof. Photograph any interior water stains, and note the date and time of the storm. This is the foundation of an insurance claim, and the earlier it's timestamped, the better.

**Stop further damage if you safely can.** If water is actively getting in, a tarp or a bucket under the leak is reasonable. Insurance policies expect you to take reasonable steps to prevent *additional* damage after an event — but don't climb up there yourself to do it. Keep receipts for any emergency materials.

**Do not sign anything with a door-knocker.** More on this below, but the single most expensive mistake homeowners make after a storm is signing a contract or an "assignment of benefits" with the first crew that shows up. Slow down.

## How to spot storm damage you can't always see from the ground

Roof storm damage is often subtle. A roof can look "fine" from the driveway and still have lost the protection it's supposed to provide. Signs worth looking for:

- **Shingles in the yard or driveway** — the clearest sign of wind damage.
- **Lifted, curled, or creased shingles.** Wind can break the seal on a shingle without tearing it off. A creased shingle has already failed even if it's still up there.
- **Granules in the gutters or at downspout splash blocks.** Heavy granule loss after hail or driving rain exposes the asphalt underneath and shortens the roof's life.
- **Dented or dinged metal** — gutters, vents, flashing, and the metal on your AC unit. Hail that dents metal has almost certainly bruised your shingles too.
- **Interior stains** on ceilings or in the attic, especially around chimneys, vents, and valleys.

**Wind vs. hail matters.** Wind damage tends to be directional and concentrated on the side the storm came from — lifted tabs, missing shingles, peeled ridge caps. Hail damage is scattered random impact marks across all slopes. Adjusters look for different things depending on the cause, which is one reason a documented professional inspection helps.

## The storm-chaser warning — read this before you sign anything

After every major storm in the region, out-of-town crews show up like seagulls at a beach picnic. They knock on doors, point at your roof from the ground, offer to "handle everything with insurance," and push you to sign on the spot. Some are legitimate. Many are very much not.

The pattern to watch for:

- **High-pressure, same-day signatures.** A real contractor will give you time and a written estimate.
- **"We'll waive your deductible" or "the roof won't cost you anything."** Waiving or absorbing your insurance deductible is **insurance fraud** in WV, MD, and VA — and a contractor who offers it is telling you exactly how they operate.
- **No local address, no verifiable license, cash-up-front demands.** When something goes wrong six months later, they're three states away.

The protection is simple: **work with a local, licensed, insured contractor you can find again.** Ask for the license number and verify it. A company that lives and works in your community has its reputation on the line in a way a traveling crew never does.

## How roof insurance claims actually work

Every policy is different, and your agent is the authority on yours — but most homeowner roof claims follow the same shape:

1. **You file the claim** with your insurer, ideally with your ground-level photos and the storm date in hand.
2. **An adjuster inspects the roof** and writes an estimate of the covered damage.
3. **Your policy pays out** based on its terms, minus your deductible.

Two terms are worth understanding before the adjuster shows up:

- **Deductible.** The amount you're responsible for before coverage kicks in. This is yours to pay — legitimately, no contractor can make it disappear.
- **ACV vs. RCV.** *Actual Cash Value* pays the depreciated value of the old roof; *Replacement Cost Value* pays to replace it new, usually in two checks — one upfront and the rest ("recoverable depreciation") once the work is completed and documented. Knowing which one your policy uses tells you what to expect.

**Where a good contractor helps:** by doing a thorough inspection, documenting the damage with photos and measurements, providing a clear written estimate, and — when it's useful — being on the roof with your adjuster so nothing legitimate gets missed. What a good contractor *won't* do is invent damage, inflate a scope, or game your deductible. An honest claim is the one that holds up.

## Repair or replace?

Not every storm means a new roof. After an inspection, the honest answer usually falls into one of three buckets:

- **A genuine repair** — a handful of missing or creased shingles on an otherwise healthy roof with years of life left. We'll tell you when that's the case.
- **Replacement** — widespread wind or hail damage, an older roof already near the end of its service life, or damage extensive enough that patching no longer makes sense.
- **A matching problem** — sometimes the damaged shingles are discontinued or weathered enough that a repair would never blend in. On a visible slope, that can tip the decision toward replacement.
