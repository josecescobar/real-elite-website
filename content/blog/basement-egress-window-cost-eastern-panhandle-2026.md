If you're a homeowner in WV's Eastern Panhandle — Martinsburg, Berkeley Springs, Charles Town, Shepherdstown — or just across the line in Maryland, the words "egress window" usually come up for one of two reasons. Either you're finishing a basement and want it to feel like real living space instead of a dark box, or you're trying to add a legal bedroom down there and just found out you can't do it without one.

Both are good reasons. This guide breaks down what an egress window actually costs in this market in 2026, what moves the price within that range, and why — if a bedroom is part of your plan — this is a line item you don't get to negotiate away.

## The headline number

A basement egress window in the Eastern Panhandle typically runs **$3,500-$6,500 installed**.

That's a real range, not a hedge — where your project lands inside it depends almost entirely on how much has to happen *outside* the window itself. A window in a spot that's already close to grade with good drainage sits at the low end. A window that requires cutting into a poured foundation well below grade, excavating a full well, and tying in new drainage sits at the top. Same window, very different job.

## What moves the price within that band

Here's what actually determines whether you're closer to $3,500 or $6,500:

- **Window well kit vs. full below-grade cut.** If the opening is relatively shallow and you're mostly dropping in a pre-made window well, the labor and materials stay modest. A deep below-grade installation — where the sill sits well under the outside grade — means a bigger well, more excavation, and more structural work.
- **Excavation.** Digging out the well means moving earth, and the Eastern Panhandle's rocky, clay-heavy ground doesn't always cooperate. Rock, tight side-yard access, or a well that has to go deep all add labor.
- **Cutting and reinforcing the foundation wall.** Opening a hole in a load-bearing foundation is the part that separates this from a normal window. Poured concrete cuts differently than block, and either way the opening has to be properly cut and reinforced so the wall stays sound. This is skilled work, not a weekend project.
- **Drainage tie-in.** A window well without drainage is a bathtub waiting to fill. Proper installations tie the well to a drainage path — often connected to the existing foundation drainage or sump system — so water moves away instead of pooling against the glass.
- **Permit and inspection.** Cutting a structural opening and adding a code-required exit means pulling a permit and passing inspection. It's part of doing the job right, and it's built into a real estimate.

None of these are optional extras dreamed up to pad a bill. They're the difference between a window that drains, seals, and holds the wall for the life of the house, and one that leaks or, worse, wasn't legal in the first place.

## This is code, not optional

If your finished basement includes a bedroom, an egress window isn't a nice-to-have — it's the law, and it's a safety requirement.

Any basement bedroom legally requires an egress window: an opening large enough for a person to climb out of in an emergency, and for a firefighter in gear to get *in*. A bedroom is a room people sleep in, and a basement bedroom with only the interior stairway has exactly one way out. If a fire starts between that room and the stairs, there is no second exit. The egress window *is* the second exit. That's the entire reason code requires it.

So if a contractor quotes you a basement bedroom without an egress window in the scope, that's not a bargain — it's a room that won't pass inspection, won't be a legal bedroom, and isn't safe to sleep in. There's no version of a legal, safe basement bedroom that skips this.

While we're on code: most jurisdictions, including Maryland, require a minimum finished ceiling height around 7 feet for habitable basement space. If your basement is tight on height, that's worth checking early, because it affects whether a bedroom is even feasible before you get to the window.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I really need an egress window if it's just a spare room, not technically a bedroom?**
If it's used for sleeping, it's a bedroom in the eyes of code, and it needs egress. A "bonus room" or "office" without a closet may sit in a gray area, but the moment you call it a bedroom — or a buyer or appraiser does — the egress requirement applies. Don't build a room you can't legally use as intended.

**Why is there such a wide gap between $3,500 and $6,500?**
Almost entirely the excavation and foundation work. A shallow window near grade in cooperative soil is a straightforward install. A deep below-grade cut through poured concrete, with heavy excavation and a new drainage tie-in, is a much bigger job. The window is often the smallest part of the cost.

**Can I add an egress window to a basement that's already finished?**
Yes, though it's more involved. We can cut in an egress window after the fact, but it means opening up finished walls, cutting the foundation from the outside, and restoring the interior. It's easier and cheaper to plan it in during a finish, but retrofitting is absolutely doable when you decide later that you want the bedroom or the light.

**Does an egress window need a permit?**
Yes. Cutting a structural opening in a foundation and adding a required exit is permitted, inspected work. A proper estimate includes the permit, and the job isn't done until the inspector signs off — not just us.
