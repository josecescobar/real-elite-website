A Loudoun County remodel doesn't move at the same pace as a remodel in the rest of the region — not because the work is harder, but because nearly every project runs through **two approval layers** instead of one. County permits and inspections are the layer most homeowners expect. HOA architectural review is the second layer that catches people off guard, adds 2–4 weeks to a typical project, and (if you don't know what you're doing) sometimes forces a redesign halfway through.

This guide is the practical map. What each layer reviews, what the real timelines look like in 2026, and how to keep both tracks running in parallel so they don't stack on top of each other.

## The two layers, side by side

| Layer | Who reviews | Typical timeline | What they care about |
|---|---|---|---|
| Loudoun County | DBPRC (Building and Development) | 2–4 weeks for most permits | Code compliance, structural safety, mechanical/electrical/plumbing, zoning |
| HOA architectural review | Community ARC (Brambleton, Lansdowne, One Loudoun, etc.) | 2–6 weeks | Exterior aesthetics, material approval, color, neighborhood consistency |

For most substantive remodels — kitchens that open a wall, bathrooms that relocate plumbing, decks of any meaningful size, additions, siding, roof color changes, window replacements — **both layers apply**. For purely interior cosmetic work (paint, cabinet refacing, tile-on-tile retile in an unchanged footprint) you usually skip both. The gray zone is everything in between, and that's where most homeowner mistakes happen.

## Layer one: Loudoun County permitting

The county side is the more predictable layer. The Department of Building & Development (DBPRC) runs an online permit portal that handles intake, fee payment, document upload, inspection scheduling, and final sign-off. Most permits move through plan review in 2–4 weeks once the application is complete. The friction points are almost always at intake — missing structural drawings, missing energy compliance documentation, missing mechanical schedules.

### Typical Loudoun permit timelines (2026)

- **Bathroom remodel** with plumbing relocation: building + plumbing + electrical permits, **2–3 weeks** for plan review.
- **Kitchen remodel** with no wall changes: building + plumbing + electrical, **2–3 weeks**.
- **Kitchen remodel** that opens a load-bearing wall: above, plus structural-engineering-stamped drawings, **3–5 weeks**.
- **Deck** of any size (in Loudoun, decks over 30 inches at any point require a permit): **2–3 weeks**.
- **Whole-home remodel** with multiple trades touching: **4–6 weeks** for the comprehensive permit package.
- **Home addition**: building + plumbing + mechanical + electrical, plus zoning review if the footprint changes. **5–8 weeks** is typical; complex sites push longer.

### Inspections you'll see

The Loudoun inspection sequence is rigorous and the order matters. For a typical full bathroom remodel: rough-in plumbing → rough-in electrical → insulation → drywall → final. For a deck: pier/footing inspection (before concrete pour) → framing inspection (before decking) → final. Skipping or rescheduling one out of order can stall a project for a week.

A good contractor coordinates the inspection sequence so the trades don't lose days waiting on each other. That's part of what you're paying for.

### When you need a structural engineer

Anytime you remove or modify a load-bearing wall, change a roof line, add to the footprint, or alter how loads transfer through the structure, you need a structural engineer's stamped drawings before the county will issue the permit. Engineering review adds 1–3 weeks on top of the permit timeline depending on the engineer's queue. We engage engineers at the same time we apply for the permit, not after — running those two timelines in parallel saves a month.

## Layer two: HOA architectural review

This is the layer that catches Loudoun homeowners off guard. Nearly every master-planned community in the county runs an Architectural Review Committee (ARC) — a small board of residents that reviews and approves exterior changes to homes in the neighborhood. The standards exist to keep the neighborhood looking consistent and protect property values, and they're real. ARCs say "no" with regularity to homeowners who submit incomplete or out-of-compliance packets.

### What each major Loudoun ARC reviews

These are the ARCs we work with most often. Each has its own submission process, its own approved-materials list, and its own meeting cadence:

- **Brambleton** — One of the largest and most active ARCs in Loudoun. Reviews all exterior changes including roofing color, siding material, paint, deck materials and railing styles, windows, doors, fences, landscaping changes, and exterior lighting. ARC meets monthly; submissions are due roughly two weeks before the meeting.

- **Lansdowne on the Potomac** — Strict on exterior aesthetics given the premium positioning of the community. Architectural details, materials, and colors all reviewed against a published design standards manual. Sometimes requires Architectural Subcommittee pre-review before full ARC submission.

- **One Loudoun** — Mixed-use community with both residential and commercial review boards. Residential exterior work goes through the residential ARC. Color palettes are tighter than in older Loudoun communities.

- **Cascades** — Long-established community with one of the more familiar review processes. Submission turnaround is typically 2–3 weeks. Standards are documented but the committee is reasonable.

- **River Creek** — Premium community along the Potomac with the same expectation for design integration as Lansdowne. Roof color changes, siding replacement, and deck additions all need approval.

- **Belmont Greene / Belmont Country Club** — Two separate but adjacent communities, each with its own ARC. Belmont Country Club ARC is the more rigorous of the two and reviews materials, colors, and architectural details closely.

- **Ashburn Farm, Broadlands, Loudoun Valley Estates** — All have active ARCs. Standards are documented; turnaround is usually 2–4 weeks.

### What a passing submission packet looks like

We've sat on the other side of an HOA review (we know what gets rejected). A packet that gets approved on the first pass typically includes:

1. **A cover letter** describing the project plainly. Scope, materials, timeline.
2. **Elevations** showing what the house will look like after — drawn or rendered. For a deck, an elevation showing the railing height and style. For siding, a full exterior elevation showing the new color.
3. **Material specifications** — manufacturer, product line, color name, profile. "Hardie ColorPlus Iron Gray in Cedar Mill profile" is what they want, not "gray fiber cement."
4. **Color samples** — printed swatches or physical samples for paint, siding, and roofing.
5. **Site plan** — for any project that changes the footprint or extends into the yard (decks, additions, patios).
6. **HOA-specific forms** — every ARC has its own application form. Use the current version; old versions get bounced.

Submissions that skip any of those almost always come back with a request for more information, costing 2–4 weeks. We've found that ARC committees are generally fair when they have everything they need to make a decision; they're not adversarial.

## Running both layers in parallel

The trap most homeowners fall into is doing the layers sequentially — apply for the HOA approval first, wait 4 weeks for it to come back, then apply for the county permit, wait another 3 weeks. That's 7+ weeks before demo starts.

The right approach is to submit both at the same time. Loudoun County doesn't require HOA approval before processing a permit application (the county doesn't enforce HOA rules; the HOA does that on its own). And the HOA generally doesn't care about the county permit. So both tracks run independently, which means they should run together.

Realistic parallel timeline for a typical Loudoun bathroom remodel:

- **Week 1:** Estimate, contract signed, design selections finalized.
- **Week 2:** County permit application submitted. HOA ARC submission packet prepared and submitted.
- **Week 3–4:** County plan review. HOA ARC meeting (assuming it falls in this window).
- **Week 4–5:** County permit issued. HOA approval received.
- **Week 5:** Demo begins.

That's 5 weeks of paperwork running in parallel, then construction starts. Compare to sequential: 4 weeks HOA → 3 weeks county → demo. That's 7 weeks of waiting for the same outcome.

## Historic district overlays

A separate consideration: **Old Town Leesburg** properties and a handful of parcels elsewhere in the county fall under historic preservation overlays. Those add a third approval layer (HARB — Historic Architectural Review Board) on top of the county permit and any HOA review. HARB reviews material authenticity, period appropriateness, and color palette. Timelines run 4–8 weeks and the standards are stricter than typical HOA review.

If you own a property in Old Town Leesburg's historic district, the planning lead time for any exterior project should start at 8 weeks of pre-construction approval work — and that's with a contractor who knows the HARB submission process.

## Common mistakes we see

A short list of the pitfalls we've watched homeowners hit:

- **Demoing before the permit is issued.** It's a tempting shortcut for a "small" remodel. Loudoun's enforcement is real; stop-work orders happen.
- **Buying materials before HOA approval.** Hardie siding in a color the ARC won't approve is a $20k mistake. Get the approval first.
- **Submitting an HOA packet without elevations.** Almost guaranteed to come back. Spend the extra hour.
- **Assuming "I'll handle the HOA myself" once construction starts.** ARC backlog and meeting schedules mean even a small request can take 4 weeks. Build it into the front of the timeline.
- **Skipping the structural engineer because "it's just a small wall."** If the wall has anything above it (a roof, a second story, even an attic), assume it's load-bearing until an engineer tells you otherwise.
