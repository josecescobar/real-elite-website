Replacing a roof is one of those decisions where the biggest call is also the most invisible — the shingle itself. For asphalt roofs (which is almost every roof in the Eastern Panhandle and the wider WV–MD–VA region), the choice basically comes down to two flavors: **3-tab** and **architectural** (a.k.a. dimensional).

Here's the straight comparison, and why Real Elite Contracting puts architectural shingles on essentially every residential roof we replace.

## The Quick Answer

For nearly every Eastern Panhandle homeowner replacing a roof in 2026: **architectural shingles.** They cost a little more upfront, then quietly outlive their 3-tab cousins by a decade, handle wind better, look dramatically nicer, and add real resale value. The price gap is small enough that 3-tab rarely pencils anymore.

3-tab still exists, and there are real cases for it. They're just narrow — like "I'm reroofing the shed" narrow.

## What's Actually Different

### 3-tab shingles

- **Flat, single-layer.** One uniform thickness, cut into three tabs per strip. Aesthetically: "builder grade."
- **Lighter:** ~200–240 lbs per square (100 sq ft).
- **Wind rating:** typically 60 mph, sometimes up to 70.
- **Lifespan:** 15–20 years realistically in our climate.
- **Warranty:** usually 25 years, prorated, and noticeably shorter in practice.

### Architectural shingles

- **Multi-layer, dimensional.** Built up in layers with a textured, shadowed look that mimics wood shake or slate. The roof actually looks like something.
- **Heavier:** 240–340+ lbs per square. More material, more durability.
- **Wind rating:** 110–130 mph, premium lines higher.
- **Lifespan:** 25–30 years realistically; premium lines longer.
- **Warranty:** 30 years to lifetime limited, depending on line and installer certification.

## Why the Climate Matters Here

The Eastern Panhandle puts roofs through a genuinely punishing schedule:

- **Wind events.** Storms rolling off the Allegheny ridges and down the valleys regularly throw 50–70 mph gusts. A 60-mph-rated 3-tab roof is living at the edge of its job description; a 110–130-mph architectural roof has actual margin.
- **Freeze-thaw.** Our winters bounce above and below freezing on repeat, which stresses every shingle. A heavier, multi-layer architectural shingle handles that beating better.
- **Sun and heat.** Summer UV slowly cooks asphalt. More material, more service life.
- **Hail and debris.** Heavier shingles resist impact better — and that one matters for insurance claims.

In this climate, the durability gap between 3-tab and architectural isn't theoretical. It's the difference between replacing your roof at year 16 and at year 28.

## The Cost Difference Is Smaller Than People Think

Homeowners often picture architectural shingles as a major upcharge. In 2026, they're not. The shingle material itself is roughly 15–25% more than 3-tab — but shingles are only one piece of a roof replacement. Tear-off, underlayment, flashing, ridge venting, disposal, and crew time cost the same either way.

On a typical Eastern Panhandle replacement, going architectural over 3-tab adds about **$800–$2,000** to the total. For that, you get 10+ extra years of life, a real wind rating, and a roof that actually flatters the house from the curb. The math favors architectural so completely it almost feels rude.

## When 3-Tab Still Makes Sense

The exceptions are real, just narrow:

1. **A rental or investment property** you're flipping in the next year or two, where the cheapest acceptable roof is the entire goal.
2. **Matching an existing 3-tab** roof on a partial replacement — mixing shingle types looks wrong, and "looks wrong" is bad on a roof.
3. **A detached structure** — a shed or outbuilding — where curb appeal and longevity matter less.

For a primary residence you plan to keep, architectural is almost always the move.

## What Actually Makes a Roof Last

Shingles matter. Installation matters more. A premium architectural shingle installed poorly will fail before a 3-tab installed well — which is why some of the saddest stories in roofing involve great materials and bad nailing. What we hold to a standard, every roof:

- **Proper underlayment** and ice-and-water shield in valleys, eaves, and around penetrations
- **Correct nailing** — right number of nails, right zone, driven right
- **Proper flashing** at chimneys, walls, valleys, and pipe penetrations
- **Ridge venting** so the attic can breathe — an under-ventilated attic cooks shingles from below like a slow oven
- **A clean tear-off** down to the deck, with any rotted sheathing replaced before we go on
