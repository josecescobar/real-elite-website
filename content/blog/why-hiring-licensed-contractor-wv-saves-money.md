## What "Licensed" Actually Means in West Virginia

West Virginia requires contractors to be licensed through the **WV Division of Labor, Contractor Licensing Division** for work above a certain dollar threshold. A licensed contractor has:

- **Passed a trade knowledge exam** proving they know the craft
- **Carried proper insurance** — general liability, plus workers' comp if they have a crew
- **Registered with the state** and accepted actual oversight
- **Agreed to follow WV building codes** instead of vibes

Requirements vary by trade — roofing, general contracting, electrical, and plumbing each have their own rules. Before you hire anyone, you can verify their license at the **WV Division of Labor's online portal**. It's free, it's fast, and it's a lot cheaper than the alternative.

## Reason 1: Unlicensed Work Can Void Your Homeowner's Insurance

This is the big one, and it ambushes people. File a homeowner's claim for damage tied to unlicensed work, and your insurer can — and frequently will — deny it.

Picture it: an unlicensed roofer does a "patch job" on your Martinsburg home for a price that felt like a win. Two years later, water intrusion causes $40,000 in mold and structural rot. You file a claim. The adjuster asks who did the prior roof work. If it wasn't a licensed contractor following code, that $40,000 may now be a *you* problem.

That "deal" just got expensive. Licensed contractors carry the insurance and follow the codes that keep your policy valid.

## Reason 2: Permits and Inspections Protect Your Sale

In West Virginia, most significant work — roof replacements over a certain scope, decks, additions, structural work — needs a permit and a local inspection.

Unlicensed contractors love skipping permits because:
- They'd rather not be watched
- They know the work won't pass
- Corners were, let's say, *generously* cut

Here's the plot twist: when you sell in Charles Town, Martinsburg, or Ranson, the buyer's home inspector **will** find unpermitted work. They always do. It's their whole personality. That can:

- Tank the sale
- Force you to pull retroactive permits — and sometimes tear out and redo the work
- Knock your price down to cover the liability

Permitted, inspected work hands you clean paperwork that quietly protects your home's value.

## Reason 3: You're Protected When Something Goes Sideways

Licensed contractors carry **general liability insurance** (covers your property if their work causes damage) and **workers' compensation** (covers you if a worker gets hurt on your property).

Hiring an unlicensed "guy who knows a guy" for a roofing job means:
- A worker falls on your property and their medical bills can land on you
- Their mistake damages your home — or the neighbor's — and you're the wallet
- They vanish mid-project and your legal options fit on a sticky note

With a licensed contractor, you have real recourse: their bond, their insurer, and the WV Division of Labor. With an unlicensed one, you mostly have a phone number that stops working.

## Reason 4: Quality and Code Compliance

Building codes aren't bureaucratic decoration — they're written in response to things that failed, sometimes dramatically. A licensed contractor knows the International Residential Code as West Virginia adopts it, including:

- Proper fastening patterns for roofing (very much your friend in an Eastern Panhandle wind event)
- Load ratings and ledger attachment for decks (the part that keeps a deck from becoming a trapdoor)
- Correct flashing and waterproofing at every penetration
- Ventilation requirements for attics and crawl spaces

An unlicensed contractor may swing a hammer beautifully and still have no formal training in code. The work can look flawless and fail the moment weather or weight tests it.

## Reason 5: Warranties That Aren't Just Decorative

Manufacturers like GAF, Owens Corning, and CertainTeed offer **extended material warranties** — but typically only when their products are installed by certified contractor networks. If an unlicensed installer puts on your shingles and they fail in year 8, the manufacturer points at the install and the warranty turns into a very nice-looking PDF.

Licensed, certified contractors give you warranties with teeth — from both the manufacturer and the contractor.

## How to Verify a WV Contractor's License

Before you sign anything:

1. **Ask for their WV contractor license number** — a real contractor rattles it off without flinching
2. **Verify it online** at the WV Division of Labor website
3. **Ask for a certificate of insurance** — actual copies of general liability and workers' comp
4. **Check reviews** on Google, Yelp, and the BBB — look for patterns, not just stars
5. **Get it in writing** — scope, materials, timeline, payment schedule, warranty

**Red flags worth trusting your gut on:**
- A bid so low it feels like a typo (something's being skipped)
- Big cash payments demanded upfront
- "This price is only good if you sign today" energy
- Can't — or won't — show a license number or insurance
- No physical address and a business history that evaporates when you Google it

If a contractor treats "what's your license number?" like a personal insult, that's your answer.
